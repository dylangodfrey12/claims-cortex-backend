### Structured Approach to Insurance Claim Supplementation

1. **Did the insurance agree to pay for damage?**  
   **Question:** Did the insurance company agree that the policy covers the type of incident that occurred?  
   - **Yes:** The insurance agreed there was damage on the property that they will pay for.  
   - **No:** If the insurance companies believe they don’t owe for damage or that the damage being claimed isn’t on the property then they will deny the claim. In this case, you must present arguments demonstrating that the damages are from wind or hail.

2. **Repairability Assessment:**  
   **Question:** Is the damage to the property repairable without further harm?  
   - **Yes:** If repairs can be completed without causing further issues to the home, you generally cannot claim additional damage resulting from the initial damage from wind or hail.  
   - **No:** If further damage is inevitable during the repair process, document this carefully and prepare to argue that repairing the damage as per the insurance company's suggestion is impracticable.

3. **Uniform Appearance Test:**  
   **Question:** Will the property look consistent and uniform after the repairs as compared to its appearance before the damage?  
   - **Yes:** If repairs will restore the property to a reasonably uniform appearance, arguments based on aesthetic inconsistency are invalid.  
   - **No:** If repairs cannot restore the property to its pre-damage uniformity, document the discrepancies and prepare arguments highlighting the loss of uniformity.

4. **Evidence Support:**  
   **Question:** Do you possess unbiased third-party evidence to support your claims?  
   - **Yes:** Document and cite this evidence in your arguments to strengthen your case. This evidence is crucial in reinforcing the legitimacy of your claims through each step of the argument.  
   - **No:** You can still proceed with your arguments, but be aware that without solid evidence, these claims may be less persuasive in legal or alternative dispute resolution settings and might lead to the denial of the claim before it reaches a courtroom or ADR.

### What Is Supplementing?

The process of submitting an estimate to an insurance carrier to charge for items not included in their original quote for repairs. These can be damaged items missed by the adjuster, materials not currently on the home but required by code, or extra work that will be required to do based on the repairs the insurance is already paying for.

### Understanding the Restoration Process in Property Damage

The concept of restoring a property to its original condition after damage involves repairing or compensating for the damage to a home to return it to the state it was in prior to the incident. In practical terms, when a property experiences damage from events like fire, theft, or natural disasters, the aim is to ensure the homeowner is not left at a disadvantage due to unexpected events.

For instance, if a homeowner’s property is damaged by fire, the company responsible will assess the damage and provide the necessary resources for repairs, thereby helping the homeowner restore the property to its condition before the damage occurred. This process excludes any deductible that the homeowner might be accountable for.

Understanding this restoration process helps homeowners navigate their repair and reimbursement claims, ensuring they know their rights and the extent of protection and support offered.

   The process of getting the insurance company to agree to cover payments for damages to your property. This means ensuring that the insurer acknowledges their responsibility to pay for damages.

   We aim to ensure that any repairs funded by the insurance company can

   Upon completing the repairs, do the new materials match the rest of the property? If not, the insurance company is responsible for replacing the entire damaged area to ensure it matches the rest of the home.

### What Is Matching

**Color Matching:**  
Refers to replacing damaged roofing/siding with new materials that closely resemble the existing color. It aims to restore the property's aesthetic appearance and ensure a seamless blend with the rest of the structure.

**Material Matching:**  
Involves replacing damaged roofing/siding with the same type or an equivalent alternative. It ensures the structural integrity, functionality, and performance of the repaired or replaced materials.

### How Will The Insurance Company Try to Respond?

Insurance companies may employ various strategies to respond to claims involving matching arguments. Most of these arguments are invalid but it is important to understand what they may be prior to encountering them so you can make a proper response.

**Argument of Non-Matching State:**  
Adjusters may contend that the damaged item does not belong to a "matching state," implying that the insurance policy does not obligate them to provide a complete replacement. They may argue that the policy only covers the repair or replacement of items that are identical to the original in terms of make, model, and condition.

- **Why is this invalid?**  
  It's invalid because, unless the policy explicitly states that they do not owe for matching, the insurance company is responsible for it regardless of the state. The reason is that they are responsible for bringing the home back to its pre-damage condition. This raises the question: if the home had a uniform color before the storm, why is it acceptable for it to have different colored materials after the repairs? Consistency in appearance is crucial to restoring the home's original aesthetic and value, ensuring that the repairs do not detract from its overall look.

**Limitation of Liability to "Like Kind and Quality":**  
Adjusters might assert that their responsibility is limited to providing compensation for items of "like kind and quality." This approach allows them to offer a replacement or repair that is similar to the damaged item but not necessarily identical, potentially limiting their financial liability.

- **This is invalid because:**  
  Many manufacturers specifically designate what materials qualify as “Like, Kind and Quality”. If we do not use the materials specified by the manufacturer, the warranty may be voided. It is essential to adhere to the manufacturer's guidelines to ensure the integrity and longevity of the repairs and to maintain the validity of the warranty.

**Proposal of "Reasonable Match":**  
Insurance companies may argue that they are not obligated to provide an exact match for the damaged item but rather a "reasonable match." This interpretation gives them more flexibility in selecting a replacement that is functionally equivalent to the original but may not have the same specific features or characteristics.

- **This is invalid because:**  
  Who determines what constitutes a reasonable match? Often, the insurance company will argue that they only owe for a reasonable match, which allows them to avoid restoring the home to its original value. As for who should determine if a match is reasonable, it should be the homeowner, as they are the ones who have to live with the colors on the property. The homeowner's preference should be paramount in ensuring satisfaction with the repairs.
